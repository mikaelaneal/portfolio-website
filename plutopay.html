PlutoPay

PlutoPay is an app that allows user to transfer money, shop, and monitor financial health without a physical bank or card.

My Process

The problem...

Users need a way to manage their shopping and finance habits online without using multiple apps or cards.

The solution...

Design a solution that allows users to do just this - manage their spending and set goals for their financial health.

Research

Understanding the problem

First, before starting any design, I looked at what already existed on the market to see where I could improve and offer something truly innovative.
I started my research by looking at two of the most popular apps that might address my problem statement: Venmo, an app that allows users to send and receive money from other users, and Dave, a tool that helps users improve their financial health.

What did I learn?
While there's already a large supply of e-wallet apps available on the market, these apps leave something to be desired by users. They tend to require users to have a bank account at a physical bank and only offer basic features, leaving a gap in the market for an e-wallet app that provides a way for users to manage their finances and improve their financial health, all in one place.

Understanding my users

With a better understanding of where I could improve what's already on the market, it's time to turn to the most important piece of UX: the user.
I conducted interviews with real potential users to get a better view of user attitudes towards major features of similar apps. But first, I needed to run a survey to guide what I would ask my target users.

Once I had a better idea of what users might need in my app, I was able to conduct my interviews with real potential users. The insight from these interviews allowed me to affinity map their attitudes and perceptions.

I consolidated all of the insights from my surveys, interviews, and affinity mapping to empathize with my users and determine what they want and need from an e-wallet app. With this data, I was able to create three user personas that address the three types of users that I've encountered.





Considering the needs and motivations of my personas, I tasked each of them with a scenario that I expect users like them to encounter in my PlutoPay app. I analyzed these scenarios on user journey maps to empathize with how users would flow through each task.

Ideation

Creating the flow

After considering what users might encounter when completing primary tasks, along with any pain points that they might experience, I created detailed user flows from the entry of the app to the completion of their desired tasks.



With the user research and my persona's goals in mind, I created a sitemap to get an overall idea of how the PlutoPay app would be laid out to create the best experience for my users.

After creating my first sitemap, I conducted a card sorting exercise to test whether my initial ideas aligned with user expectations. I allowed participants to create and name their own categories to get a better understanding of what they expect. This informed the final sitemap.

Wireframing

Bringing PlutoPay to life

After finalizing the sitemap, I created a low-fidelity prototype to decide how the three primary functions of my app would work for my target users. These functions are transferring money, filtering transactions and using the PlutoPay card number to shop online.



After validating the flow of my main tasks with my low-fidelity prototypes, I was able to begin creating higher fidelity prototypes for usability testing. Here's a few screens from the mid-fidelity prototype.





Design and Iteration

Testing the waters and making improvements

I began testing some of my designs to make sure that the basic functionality was intuitive and enjoyable for users.
The goal of this testing was to identify any errors in the basic functionality of the app by testing the completion of the three primary tasks that we expect users to complete using the PlutoPay app:
Transfer money
View spending habits
Shop online with the PlutoPay card
6 usability tests were performed following a detailed plan and errors were measured using Jakob Nielsen’s rating scale.
First, I organized the results into errors, observations, and positive or negative quotes:​​​​​​

As I rounded out the functionality, I also started working on preference testing to see what users might like to see in the app. I created 3 mood boards to determine what scheme the app would follow.



From about 30 participants, the first mood board was the most popular.
From here, I used Gestalt Laws of Grouping and Visual Design Principles to provide a better, more enjoyable user experience.
With the choice of the color scheme of the app and the design principles I decided to follow, I was able to create a style guide to maintain consistency across the remainder of the app, including color schemes, typography, UI elements, grid layouts, imagery, and the tone of voice used in the app.
